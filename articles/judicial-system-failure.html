Yes the judicial system failed and so did we!

By Radha Jha • March 2026

Every time a headline breaks about a rape or a murder, we suddenly transform into activists. But what happens the rest of the time?

We protest for justice and chant slogans for a few days, only to retreat to our daily routines once the fervour dies down. The moment we return from a protest, we lapse back into unconscious habits, using abusive language and sharing sexist jokes. We find it easy to share a story for a victim on social media, yet we lack the resolve to correct our own behaviour or challenge the people in our inner circles.

If the issue were solely a 'deaf system,' why did the teacher protests of 2082 last for months while our outcries against violence end with a resigned 'that’s just how it is'?

We only seem to take a stand when it directly benefits us. We demand that authorities execute justice, yet we fail to practice justice ourselves. Every fiscal year in Nepal, over 3,000 cases are registered, while thousands more are silenced. As a society, we are often the first to make a victim feel uncomfortable, and that is the very first injustice they face.

We act like activists, but how often do we actually silence our friends, relatives, or family members when they make vulgar or racist jokes? Those jokes, and our silence upon hearing them, are the true reflections of who we are.

The next time we hear a 13-year-old using abusive language, let’s make sure to correct them. That child might or might not listen, but as responsible citizens, we must remember that their current mentality could very well lead us toward the next protest for a victim's justice. If we don't intervene now, we are simply waiting for the next tragedy to occur.

The next time we find ourselves lecturing someone on how to stay safe and protect themselves, let’s also make sure we are teaching others how to behave with integrity and not cause harm. True safety doesn't come from better defences; it comes from a society that refuses to produce more offenders.

These words may sound like an idealistic concept, but the fact that it isn't our reality now doesn't mean it can never be achieved.

So does our activism end with a 'share' button on social media, or does it live in the rooms where we risk being labelled 'too rebellious' or 'just another feminist'?
