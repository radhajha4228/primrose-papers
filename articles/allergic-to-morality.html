“SORRY! I AM ALLERGIC TO MORALITY”

By Radha Jha • March 2026

Every time I acknowledge a social issue or someone’s problem, all people say is, "But that’s their problem, not ours. It's better if they solve it themselves."

If talking about social issues isn't our responsibility, then why is their personal life our 'daily tea'? For instance, you’ll gossip about the neighbor’s son smoking near the market. As a shopkeeper, you think selling cigarettes to a minor is 'just business,' yet you don't seem to 'mind your own business' when a girl walks into your shop wearing shorts.

When a husband beats his wife to the point of death, why, as a 'responsible' neighbor, you refuse to inform the police? Yet, you feel responsible enough to criticize someone for marrying a partner with a physical disability.

If you are so worried about a child that doesn't even exist yet, why aren't you worried about the woman who is bleeding right in front of you?

When a woman accepts patriarchy and abuse, you say, 'That’s her choice. If she doesn't speak up, why should you care?' But wouldn't you criticize me if I dressed my toddler in old, torn clothes? You would speak up for the child because she can't speak for herself. Why, then, do you refuse to speak for victims of abuse who have been silenced?

Welcome to a society where we preach morality and justice with blood on our hands. We call ourselves 'social beings,' yet we have forgotten the purpose of society: to protect one another, not to police one another.

“कभी प्यासे को पानी पिलाया नहीं, बाद अमृत पिलाने से क्या फायदा, कभी गिरते हुए को उठाया नही, बाद आंसू बहाने से क्या फायदा”

This song is exactly what I feel when I see people wailing at funerals. I know you didn't offer a single drop of empathy when they were alive and desperate, yet now that they are gone, you offer a million tears. If you have that much water to waste, go cry over a famine-stricken land where it might actually do some good.

I know the answer: you will keep asking her to stay calm until she is raped or killed. Because only once a tragedy occurs are you ready to post your social media stories saying, 'How sad! Kalyug is at its peak.' Your 'concern' only wakes up when it’s too late to be useful.

How long will we pretend to fight against corruption when we secretly love the benefits it brings us? We feel a surge of pride when we can bypass the system through a politician’s favor, yet the moment we lack that 'power-help,' we become the loudest voice for equality and justice.

Are you a voice for change, or are you the reason change still needs a voice?
