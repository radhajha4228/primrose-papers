Why Fix an Unbroken Vessel?

Published April 2026 • By Radha Jha

For as long as I remember have been told to ‘fix’ the bumps on my arms and the texture of my face, I have always seen them as part of me. They harm neither my skin nor my self-esteem.

Yet, it seems every person I meet is a self-appointed dermatologist or psychologist, eager to repair a problem that doesn’t exist. I never needed a suggestion or a cream to feel ‘fine’ because these traits were never problematic to me.

"As humans, we often struggle to confess our own insecurities, so we try to fix others instead."

At every event or function, you meet countless people ready with suggestions to ‘fix’ your skin or hair, when the thing that actually needs fixing is their own self-confidence. We love suggesting creams to make skin ‘flawless,’ forgetting that nature itself embraces roughness, bumps, and the little differences that make each of us unique.

It is a strange human obsession to crave uniformity. We don’t ask for fingerprints to be smooth or patternless, nor do we expect the human form to be as symmetrical as a cuboid. Yet, when it comes to skin and hair, we treat every natural variation as a flaw instead of a signature.

People’s love for flawless skin and shiny hair is justifiable—it is their choice. Similarly, embracing our own skin and hair is our choice. Yet, people still try to reshape us, as if we were nothing more than clay on a potter’s wheel.

Sometimes, we simply need to hold our tongues when we encounter a different skin tone, a unique texture, or a choice we don’t understand. We may not share their preference, but we must acknowledge their happiness. If you cannot find something to admire, you do not need to point out the weight gained or the hair lost.

We are not projects to be finished; we are people to be respected.

Thank you for reading this inquiry. Perspective is the first step toward justice.
