Luxury is Not the Charm of Pain

Published March 2026 • By Radha Jha

We have heard it a thousand times from brands and retailers: "The product is expensive because the raw materials are rare." Or, "Prices have risen because the cost of initial materials is high."

But is that the full truth? If expensive raw materials truly dictated the wealth of the producer, the villagers in the mines and the laborers in the fields would be thriving—or at the very least, living with dignity. Instead, they remain trapped in poverty.

"The 'luxury' price tag exists primarily to benefit the corporation and the high-status consumer, rarely reaching the 'inner core' workers who make the product possible."

Millions of children toil in mines to provide the minerals for the jewelry we wear and the technology we carry. We buy the latest phones and laptops, using those very devices to post about ending child labor. We advocate for "sustainable development," yet the concept of a "sustainable life" for the worker is ignored.

We mindlessly consume "fast fashion," only to dump it in bins while complaining about waste management. I am not suggesting we abandon all luxury. However, we must ask ourselves: Is the luxury you enjoy the result of your own hard work, or is it built upon someone else’s pain?

Before we buy, we should consider whether our comfort is fueled by the struggle of a child simply trying to fill their stomach.

Thank you for reading this inquiry. Knowledge is a form of rebellion.
